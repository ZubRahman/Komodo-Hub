Endangered Species

Learn about the world's most vulnerable wildlife and the conservation efforts to protect them.

Vulnerable

Komodo Dragon

The largest living lizard species, native to the Indonesian islands of Komodo, Rinca, Flores, and Gili Motang.

Scientific Name: Varanus komodoensis
Population: Approximately 3,000-4,000
Habitat: Tropical savanna forests and grasslands
Threats: Habitat loss, poaching, natural disasters
Vulnerable

Giant Panda

The giant panda is a bear native to China, known for its distinctive black and white coat and specialized diet consisting almost entirely of bamboo.

Scientific Name: Ailuropoda melanoleuca
Population: Approximately 1,800 in the wild
Habitat: Temperate forests with dense bamboo undergrowth
Threats: Habitat fragmentation, limited food resources
Critically Endangered

Sumatran Tiger

The Sumatran tiger is a tiger subspecies native to the Indonesian island of Sumatra and is the smallest of all tiger subspecies.

Scientific Name: Panthera tigris sumatrae
Population: Fewer than 400 individuals
Habitat: Tropical rainforests and peat swamp forests
Threats: Poaching, habitat loss due to palm oil plantations
Critically Endangered

Black Rhinoceros

The black rhinoceros is a species of rhinoceros native to eastern and southern Africa, distinguished by its hooked upper lip.

Scientific Name: Diceros bicornis
Population: Approximately 5,500 individuals
Habitat: Semi-desert savanna, woodlands, and forests
Threats: Poaching for horns, habitat loss
Critically Endangered

Hawksbill Sea Turtle

The hawksbill sea turtle is a critically endangered sea turtle belonging to the family Cheloniidae, known for its beautiful shell.

Scientific Name: Eretmochelys imbricata
Population: Unknown, but severely depleted
Habitat: Coral reefs and coastal waters in tropical oceans
Threats: Illegal wildlife trade, bycatch in fishing gear, habitat destruction
Endangered

Blue Whale

The blue whale is a marine mammal and the largest animal known to have ever existed, reaching lengths of up to 30 meters.

Scientific Name: Balaenoptera musculus
Population: 10,000-25,000 worldwide
Habitat: All major oceans, preferring deep waters
Threats: Ship strikes, entanglement in fishing gear, ocean noise
